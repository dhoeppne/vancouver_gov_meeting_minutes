RECOMMENDATIONS
A. THAT the Vancouver Park Board approve, as a pilot program, the requirements of the

PURPOSE AND SUMMARY
The Vancouver Park Board introduced a no smoking policy in parks and beaches in July 2010 to
promote a healthy urban environment and support active living and wellness. While policy
compliance is generally positive amongst most day-to-day park users, illicit smoking continues
to be an issue of concern at large events and gatherings. Designated smoking areas have shown
to be effective in significantly reducing illicit smoking at events and are now being introduced by
many cities to remedy this issue.
The purpose of this report is to present the concept of designated smoking areas at events to
the Park Board, review requests from two event organizers (Trixstar Productions and Feaster
Festivals & Events) to implement this approach (tobacco and vaping products only) at their
events in Summer 2026 on a pilot program basis, and seek the Board’s approval for this request.
BOARD AUTHORITY / PREVIOUS DECISIONS
Per the Vancouver Charter, the Park Board has exclusive jurisdiction and control over parkland
use in the City of Vancouver (including any structures, programs and activities, fees, and
improvements within parks) and approves the use of all lands under its jurisdiction on such terms
as the Park Board may decide.
On June 22, 2010, the Board approved the Park Board Smoking Regulation Bylaw to regulate
smoking in parks and also approved the Ticket Offences Bylaws to permit enforcement of Park
bylaws by way of municipal tickets.
Per the Parks Control Bylaws, Park Board approval is required for divergences or exemptions to
these Bylaws.

CONTEXT AND BACKGROUND
Parks Control Bylaw and No-Smoking Policy
The Vancouver Park Board is responsible for stewarding more than 240 parks and nearly 18
kilometers of beaches in the city. These open spaces are used by millions of people and offer
residents and visitors of all ages valuable opportunities for recreation and relaxation.
The Vancouver Parks Control Bylaw (Bylaw No. 5655) outlines the rules, regulations, and
restrictions governing all public parks and beaches managed by the Park Board. It dictates the
parameters of public use to maintain order, safety, and cleanliness.
Following the introduction of restrictions on smoking in outdoor public spaces in numerous
jurisdictions across North America, and aligned to the Park Board’s commitment to promote a
healthy urban environment and support for active living and wellness, a no smoking policy was
approved by the Park Board on July 5, 2010. This new regulation applies to all parks, including
pathways, playgrounds, playing surfaces contained within each, as well as all city beaches.
Special Events
Civic open spaces such as parks, plazas, and beaches, as well as admission-based facilities
including VanDusen Botanical Garden and Bloedel Conservatory, are popular sites for
entertainment and celebrations of all natures and sizes. The Park Board’s role in supporting
special events is primarily that of a host, issuing over 1,500 permits annually for special events
and other activities across its open space network and facilities; the Park Board also organizes
several events for the benefit and enjoyment of Vancouverites and visitors to the city.
Special events play an essential role in building community connection, fostering diversity and
cohesion, driving economic activity through tourism and support for local businesses, promoting
arts, sports and culture, and providing opportunities for education, awareness and civic
engagement.
Accordingly, Park Board staff work collaboratively with event organizers to enable events,
improve the efficiency of operations, and minimize/reduce potential risks.
What is the Issue?
While significant effort is taken to prevent illegal smoking at events, illicit smoking is frequent at
large public gatherings, especially music festivals and outdoor concerts. Surveys indicate that
up to 37% of festival-goers smoke – roughly double the rate of the general public – leading to
widespread, routine rule-breaking at non-smoking events.
Illegal smoking at events usually happens due to limited security resources, crowd density
making it easy for attendees to hide their actions, the discreet nature of vapes, and municipal
bylaw reliance on voluntary compliance rather than active enforcement/ticketing. Because event
staff cannot monitor every person, attendees often exploit these gaps to circumvent smoking
bans.
Common ways illegal smoking happens at events includes:
− Secluded & dark areas – People migrate to poorly lit zones, backstage corridors, or
dense, hidden areas where security visibility is minimal;
− Restroom stalls – Bathrooms remain a primary location for breaking smoking rules, as
attendees can lock themselves in a stall to consume cigarettes and other products;

Why is this needed?
Designated smoking areas at events, when effectively managed, signposted, and supported by
active security enforcement, help reduce illicit smoking while simultaneously improving public
safety and enhancing park protection (particularly during the dry summer months and noting that
locally, Metro Vancouver implemented Stage 2 water restrictions on May 1 (skipping Stage 1
altogether) in the anticipation of a hot, dry summer with threat of drought). Event organizers have
consistently observed that offering clearly delineated smoking zones decreases illicit smoking
throughout the broader venue footprint due to:
− Clear compliance – Smokers prefer having an accessible, authorized place to smoke or
vape. Providing convenient designated zones – such as the fenced-in areas successfully
used at summer festivals – decreases the likelihood of attendees lighting up illegally at
events.
− Safer alternatives – At public events, designated smoking locations naturally encourage
attendees to consume in designated areas, lowering the risk of second-hand smoke
exposure in densely packed, family-oriented spaces.
− Municipal regulations – In municipalities across Metro Vancouver public spaces are strictly
smoke-free. Offering contained, permitted zones prevents attendees from stepping just
outside event boundaries to smoke illegally or violate municipal by-laws.
Ultimately, while strict, completely smoke-free rules require rigorous oversight, pairing prohibitions
with designated smoking areas – along with proper enforcement and event public health
regulations and fines – has been shown to promote significantly better overall compliance.
Considering these proven benefits and results, multiple cities across Canada that restrict smoking
in outdoor public spaces have supported the introduction of designated smoking areas for special
events in their communities, including Surrey, Kelowna, Edmonton, Calgary, Winnipeg,
Mississauga, Toronto, Montreal, and Halifax. Locally, the PNE also permits smoking within
designated areas.
Liquor as a Past Precedent for Bylaw Exemptions
In recent years (between May 2021 and May 2024), the Park Board has approved a series of
amendments to the Parks Control Bylaw to designate specific locations where liquor may be
consumed in parks. Prior to these amendments, however, Park Bylaws explicitly restricted the
consumption of liquor in park spaces, unless expressly approved by the Board. As part of the
special event approval process, the Park Board regularly approves designated areas for liquor
consumption in parks falling outside of these designated areas. The proposed pilot program in
this report seeks to follow a similar model whereby designated smoking areas for events would

Compliance with Park Board Bylaws
Consistent with all events taking place in Park Board spaces, the event organizer would be

Event Benefits
Staff consider requests of this nature very carefully and wish to emphasize that advancing this
initiative does not represent an endorsement or support for the consumption of tobacco
products/validation of the smoking culture. Rather, staff are supportive of pursuing this pilot
program due to the anticipated benefits which include:
− Reduced illegal smoking/improved Park Bylaw compliance (either within or adjacent to the
event location);
− Improved public safety;
− Improved park protection during the dry summer season;
− Operational efficiencies for event organizers (resources can be focussed rather than
dispersed around the event area); and
− Improved guest experience.
FINANCIAL CONSIDERATIONS
All expenses related to the delivery and execution of a designated smoking areas at the events
identified in this report would be borne by the event producers; there will be no cost to the Park
Board related to this initiative.
OTHER CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed pilot program is supported by the Vancouver Police Department, Vancouver Fire
Rescue Services, and the City of Vancouver’s Public Safety and Risk Management teams due to
the potential for improved bylaw compliance as well as the public and risk reduction benefits.
Park Board staff will work with the organizers of these events to collect observations and data
related to this initiative to support a report back to the Board no later than March 31, 2027.
Park Board staff acknowledge that advancing this pilot program could present some potential
organizational reputational risks and/or be misinterpreted as support for the consumption of
tobacco products/validation of the smoking culture. It is believed that these risks/issues can be
mitigated through appropriate communications and data sharing.
Lastly, and consistent with current practices, this initiative would be subject to a review and Board
report back before designated smoking areas are introduced at these or other events in the future.
